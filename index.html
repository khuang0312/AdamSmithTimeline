Timeline

January 16th, 1723

Born in Kirkcaldy, Scotland to Adam Smith and Margaret Douglas. Sadly, his father died two months before his birth.

June 5th, 1723

Baptized at the Church of Scotland at Kirkcaldy.

1726

Abducted by Romani but released when others came to rescue him according to the Scottish journalist John Rae.

1729 to 1737

Attended the Burgh School of Kirkcaldy, learning Latin, mathematics, history, and writing.

1737

Entered the University of Glasgow, studying moral philosophy under Francis Hutcheson, developing a passion for liberty, reason, and free speech.

1740

Goes on to Balliol College at Oxford under the Snell Exhibition, a scholarship.

1746

Suffering shaking fits, he leaves Oxford before his scholarship ends. According to his letters, he did not seem to enjoy Oxford.

1748

Began publicly lecturing at the University of Edinburgh with a sponsorship by the Philosophical Society of Edinburgh on the topics of rhetoric, belles-lettres, and "the progress of opulence".

1750

Met the philosopher David Hume.

1751

Earned a professorship at Glasgow University teaching logic courses.

1752

Elected to the Philosophical Society of Edinburgh and took over as head of Moral Philosophy in Glasgow the next year after the previous head died.

1759

Published The Theory of Moral Sentiments, discussing how human morality depends on sympathy between society and individuals, coining the term "mutual sympathy" which is best summed up as empathy.

1762

Received the title of Doctor of Laws from The University of Glasgow.

1763

Charles Townshend hires Smith to teach his stepson, Henry Scott, the young Duke of Buccleuch. Smith resigns partway through the next year, attempting to return fees to his students. However, they refused because they greatly enjoyed his lectures.

1764 to 1766

Touring Europe with Scott, Smith travels to Paris where he meets the Physiocrats who argue France should focus solely on agriculture since it maintained the country's wealth despite mercantilist ventures and wars abroad.

This stark comparison between productive and unproductive labor inspires Smith. The tour ends shortly after Scott's younger brother's death.

1773

Smith is elected fellow of the Royal Society of London.

1776

Publishes The Wealth of Nations, selling out its first edition in only six months.

1778

Appointed to a post as commissioner of customs in Scotland.

1783

Becomes one of the founding members of the Royal Society of Edinburgh.

1787 to 1789

Occupies the honorary position of Lord Rector of the University of Glasgow.

July 17th, 1790

Dies in the northern wing of Panmure House in Edinburgh after a painful illness.
